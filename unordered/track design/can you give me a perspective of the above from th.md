# Ballasted Track Engineering: ADIF, Network Rail, and HS2 Standards Perspective

Based on analysis of HS2 Technical Standard HS2-HS2-RT-STD-000-000004, ADIF specifications, and Network Rail standards, here is a comprehensive comparison of the three major railway infrastructure managers' approaches to ballasted track design.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5]

***

## 1. Ballast and Subballast Layer Thicknesses

### 1.1 HS2 (High Speed 2 - UK)

**Ballast Depth Requirements:**[^1]

- **Minimum ballast depth (measured between protection layer and sleeper soffit):**
    - Earthworks: **300 mm** (standard), **250 mm** (depots and sidings)
    - Structures (bridges): **400 mm**
    - Piled slab: **450 mm**
- **Ballast shoulders:** Extend **1100 mm** outwards from running edge of rail, in line with top of sleeper[^1]
- **Ballast profile:** Top of ballast level is **40 mm below top of sleeper** to reduce ballast flight risk[^1]
- **Maximum first layer depth:** 180 mm, stabilised in 100 mm maximum layer depths thereafter[^1]

**Protection Layer (Sub-ballast):**[^1]

- **Material:** Bitumen asphalt (innovative approach for high-speed)
    - Earthworks: **120 mm** thick
    - Structures: **50 mm** thick
- **Performance:** Provides uniform stiffness, low deformation, waterproofing, and protects sub-grade[^6]
- **Alternative:** Granular fill for depots and maintenance facilities[^1]


### 1.2 ADIF (Spain)

**Ballast Depth Requirements:**[^2][^4]

- **High-speed lines (≥200 km/h):**
    - Ballast: **30-35 cm** (increased 5 cm above UIC 1989 methodology)
    - Sub-ballast: **30 cm**
    - Total track bed: **60-65 cm**[^2]
- **Minimum values:**[^2]
    - Ballast: **25 cm** (general), **30 cm** if speed >120 km/h
    - Sub-ballast: **25 cm** if speed >160 km/h
    - Track bed: **60 cm** if speed >200 km/h

**Sub-ballast Layer:**[^2]

- Typically crushed rock material
- Minimum **25 cm** for high-speed (>160 km/h)
- **30 cm** standard for high-speed lines based on UIC methodology with safety factor[^2]


### 1.3 Network Rail (UK)

**Ballast Depth Requirements:**[^3][^7]

- **Minimum ballast depth:** Varies by line type and loading (standard NR/L2/TRK/2102)[^5]
    - Typical: **250-300 mm** below sleeper soffit
    - High-speed main lines: **300 mm**
    - Secondary lines: Can be reduced based on traffic assessment[^7]
- **Design life:** Approximately **25 years** on high-speed main line, up to **50 years** on secondary rural lines with appropriate maintenance[^7]

**Formation Layer (Sub-ballast):**[^7]

- Typically sand blanket (blanketing layer)
- Thickness variable based on ground conditions and traffic load
- Distributes dynamic load over sub-grade[^7]

**Comparison Summary - Layer Thicknesses:**

| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Ballast depth (earthworks)** | 300 mm | 300-350 mm | 250-300 mm |
| **Ballast depth (structures)** | 400-450 mm | N/A | Variable |
| **Sub-ballast type** | Bitumen asphalt | Crushed rock | Sand blanket |
| **Sub-ballast thickness** | 120 mm (earth) / 50 mm (structure) | 300 mm | Variable |
| **Total construction depth** | 710 mm (standard ballasted) | 600-650 mm | Variable |
| **Ballast shoulder width** | 1100 mm | N/A specified | Variable |

## 2. Ballast Specifications and Properties

### 2.1 Material Requirements

**HS2 Specifications:**[^1]

- **Material:** Crushed rock from siliceous, igneous, and metamorphic sources
- **Particle size:** **31.5-50 mm** (Category A of BS EN 13450)[^1]
- **Los Angeles coefficient:** **≤14** (most stringent requirement globally)[^1]
- **Water absorption:** **≤0.5%**[^1]
- **Washing required:** All ballast must be washed **prior to being laid** in track[^1]
    - Washing at source insufficient - fines build up during transport and storage[^1]
- **Gluing prohibited:** Ballast gluing is explicitly prohibited[^1]

**ADIF Specifications:**[^4]

- **Three ballast types:**
    - **Type "1":** High-speed systems (≥200 km/h)
    - **Type "2":** Conventional network (<200 km/h, can be used for mechanized maintenance of high-speed if tamping only)
    - **Type "3":** Narrow gauge lines (<1436 mm) and Class "C" lines
- **Particle size:** **22.4-63 mm**[^4]
- **Minimum thickness:** **25 mm** per particle[^4]
- **Los Angeles coefficient:** Varies by type (Type 1 most stringent)
- **Permitted percentage between 25-16 mm:** Maximum **27%** (formula: C ≤ 39.5 - CLA)[^4]
- **Material:** Must come from healthy, hard, resistant rock with adequate physical properties[^4]

**Network Rail Specifications:**[^8]

- **Standard:** NR/SP/TRK/006 "Railway Ballast \& Stoneblower Aggregate"[^8]
- Applies to all Network Rail controlled infrastructure except ballast returned by cleaning machines[^8]
- Follows BS EN 13450 with additional Network Rail requirements
- **High-speed specification:** LA coefficient ≤14 (aligned with HS2)[^6]


### 2.2 Testing and Quality Control

**HS2 Testing Protocol:**[^1]

- Compliance with **BS EN 13450 Category A**
- Washing verification tests
- Particle size distribution analysis
- Los Angeles test (≤14)
- Water absorption test (≤0.5%)

**ADIF Testing Protocol:**[^4]

- Los Angeles wear resistance test
- Particle flakiness coefficient
- Weathering resistance (boiling in saline solution)
- Impact resistance
- Minimum thickness measurement
- Quality control at production facility and on-site

**Network Rail Testing:**[^8]

- BS EN 13450 compliance
- Additional Network Rail specific tests
- Regular sampling and testing regime
- Quality assurance at approved quarries

## 3. Sleeper Specifications

### 3.1 HS2 Requirements

**Sleeper Type:**[^1]

- **Mandatory:** Pre-stressed concrete mono-block type only
- **Design:** Bespoke designs specific to HS2 requirements (per BS EN 13230-1, BS EN 16730, INF TSI)

**Specifications:**[^1]

- **Minimum compressive strength:** C50/60 MPa
- **Minimum sleeper weight:** **300 kg**
- **Length:** **2600 mm**
- **Depth under rail seat:** **220 mm**
- **Minimum sleeper base area:** **790,000 mm²**
- **Sleeper spacing:** **600 mm** center-to-center (mainline), **650 mm** (depot/maintenance facilities)
- **Design feature:** Smaller cross-section in central portion to allow bending flexure[^1]

**Under Sleeper Pads (USPs):**[^1]

- **Mandatory** for all sleepers and S\&C bearers
- **Standard:** BS EN 16730, IRS 70713-1
- **Nominal thickness:** **8-10 mm**

```
- **Type:** Stiff category with bedding modulus C<sub>stat</sub> between **0.25-0.35 N/mm³**
```

- **Installation:** Cast into sleeper soffit during manufacturing (must not protrude beyond sleeper edges)
- **Service life:** Equal to sleeper life
- **Proven for high-speed:** Required evidence of high-speed line performance[^1]


### 3.2 ADIF Requirements

**Sleeper Standards:**[^2]

- Predominantly pre-stressed concrete monoblock
- Twin-block sleepers used in specific applications
- Dimensions vary by line type and design speed
- **Typical high-speed:** 2.6 m length for standard gauge (1435 mm)[^2]

**Under Sleeper Pads:**

- Increasingly used on high-speed lines
- Specifications aligned with European standards
- Applied to reduce ballast pressure and improve track geometry stability


### 3.3 Network Rail Requirements

**Sleeper Types:**[^3]

- Mix of concrete, timber (legacy), and steel sleepers depending on route and application
- **Concrete sleepers:** Standard for high-speed and main lines
- **Timber sleepers:** Still in use on secondary routes
- Gradual migration to concrete sleepers for renewals

**Specifications:**

- Various approved designs depending on line speed and loading
- Spacing typically 650 mm on high-speed routes
- **Under Sleeper Pads:** Increasingly specified for new installations and renewals on critical routes[^3]

| **Spacing** | 600 mm (mainline) | Variable | 650 mm (high-speed) |
| **USPs mandatory** | Yes (all sleepers) | Increasingly required | Increasingly specified |
| **USP thickness** | 8-10 mm | Variable | Variable |
| **Compressive strength** | C50/60 MPa | Variable | Variable |

## 4. Fastener Specifications

### 4.1 HS2 Requirements

**Fastening System:**[^1]

- **Standard:** BS EN 13481-2 (for concrete sleepers)
- **Proven performance:** Must be demonstrated in high-speed operation
- **Efficient maintenance:** Must facilitate efficient maintenance and renewal, particularly rail replacement[^1]
- **Relaxation:** Requirements may be relaxed where speeds are ≤250 km/h if proven for location design speed[^1]

**Functional Requirements:**[^1]

- Rail creep force compliance per EN 13481
- Maintain track gauge under operational loads
- Facilitate rail adjustment for geometry corrections
- Electrical insulation (where required)
- **Key feature:** Must enable efficient automated maintenance operations


### 4.2 ADIF Requirements

**Fastening Systems:**[^2]

- Variety of approved fastening systems depending on line type
- **High-speed lines:** Predominantly elastic fastenings (e.g., Vossloh systems, Pandrol)
- **Conventional lines:** Mix of elastic and traditional fastenings
- Compliance with European standards (EN 13481 series)

**Specifications:**

- Must maintain track gauge under maximum design loads
- Provide adequate electrical insulation for signaling systems
- Facilitate tamping and maintenance operations
- Proven durability under Spanish climate conditions


### 4.3 Network Rail Requirements

**Fastening Standards:**[^9]

- **Modern installations:** Predominantly elastic fastenings (Pandrol e-Clip, Vossloh systems)
- **Legacy systems:** Many older fastening types still in service
- Compliance with BS EN 13481 series

**Key Requirements:**

- Maintain track gauge within specified tolerances
- Adequate rail restraint for long welded rail
- Corrosion resistance for UK climate
- Compatibility with existing infrastructure during renewals

## 5. Track Stiffness and Performance Requirements

### 5.1 HS2 Track Stiffness Targets

**Global Track Stiffness:**[^1]

- **Standard ballasted track:** **100 kN/mm ±20%**
- **Depot ballasted track:** **60 kN/mm ±10%**
- **Vertical rail deflection limit:** **≤1.3 mm** in operation (standard ballasted track)[^1]

**Design Philosophy:**[^6][^1]

- **Too stiff:** Components deteriorate quickly due to higher stresses
- **Too soft:** Wheel-rail interface issues and geometric faults develop
- **Optimal target:** Balance between component stress and geometric stability
- **Stiffness transitions:** Must not exceed **0.5 mm rail deflection** change over transition length[^1]

**Sub-grade Requirements:**[^1]

- **Ev2 modulus:** **120 MPa** minimum (new track), **100 MPa** (upgraded track)

```
- **Proctor density:** D<sub>pr</sub> ≥0.98-1.0 to depth of 3 m below sleeper base[^1]
```


### 5.2 ADIF Performance Criteria

**Platform Requirements:**[^2]

- **Ev2 modulus (protection layer surface):** ≥120 MN/m²
- **Platform depth:** Minimum 60 cm for speeds >200 km/h
- **Load distribution:** Based on UIC methodology with Spanish adaptations
- **Settlement limits:** Strict control for high-speed applications


### 5.3 Network Rail Performance Standards

**Track Bed Requirements:**[^7]

- Variable track bed stiffness management critical
- **Desirable:** Consistent stiffness along route to prevent differential settlement
- **Problem areas:** Identified through track geometry monitoring and GPR surveys[^7]
- **Interventions:** Track bed strengthening where modulus deficiencies identified[^7]

**Comparison Summary - Performance:**


| Parameter | HS2 | ADIF | Network Rail |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Track stiffness (ballasted)** | 100 kN/mm ±20% | Variable | Variable |
| **Rail deflection limit** | ≤1.3 mm | Variable | Variable |
| **Ev2 (sub-grade)** | 120 MPa (new) | 120 MN/m² | Variable |
| **Stiffness transition control** | 0.5 mm/transition | Variable | Managed |
| **Sub-grade depth requirement** | Dpr ≥0.98 to 3m | Variable | Variable |

## 6. Installation and Quality Control

### 6.1 HS2 Installation Requirements

**Ballast Laying:**[^1]

- **Layer installation:** Maximum **180 mm** first layer, then **100 mm** maximum layers
- **Consolidation method:** Dynamic Track Stabiliser (DTS) - vertical vibratory force tools (whacker plates) **prohibited**[^1]
- **Groove cutting:** Nominal **150 mm** wide × **50 mm** deep groove in initial ballast layer before sleeper placement to prevent centre-bound cracking[^1]
- **Process:** Tamper → ballast regulator → DTS typically requires **7-8 passes** until acceptance criteria met[^1]

**Quality Acceptance:**[^1]

- **Static measurements:** Track position ±5 mm absolute, ±2 mm longitudinal/lateral level (10m basis)
- **Dynamic measurements:** Under minimum 80 kN wheel load at speeds up to 160 km/h
- **Rail adjustment reserve at acceptance:**
    - Vertical: ≤5 mm of adjustment capacity used
    - Horizontal: ≤1 mm of adjustment capacity used
- **Rail grinding:** ≤1 mm material removal to achieve quality[^1]


### 6.2 ADIF Installation Standards

**Construction Process:**[^2]

- Layered construction approach
- Compaction to specified density standards
- Quality control at each stage of construction
- GPS-based positioning for high-speed lines
- Comprehensive testing regime before handover

**Acceptance Criteria:**

- Strict geometric tolerances for high-speed lines
- Dynamic testing under load
- Long-term settlement monitoring before full service speed authorization


### 6.3 Network Rail Installation Practice

**Construction Standards:**[^7]

- Follows NR/L2/TRK/2102 for design and construction[^5]
- Layered installation approach
- Quality assurance through approved contractors
- Site-specific acceptance criteria based on route importance

**Monitoring:**[^7]

- Track geometry monitoring systems
- Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) for ballast condition assessment
- Rolling Standard Deviation analysis for deterioration rate prediction[^7]

## 7. Maintenance Philosophy Comparison

### 7.1 HS2 Maintenance Strategy

**Design Life Targets:**[^1]

- **Track system:** Minimum **60 years** (not applicable to rail replacement)
- **Ballast (expected):** ~20 years before renewal required at high tonnage (>60 MGTPA)[^10]
- **Philosophy:** Minimize maintenance through optimal initial design rather than reactive maintenance[^1]

**Maintenance Access:**[^1]

- **Maximum overnight window:** 5 hours (8 hours weekends)
- **Design principle:** All components capable of repair/renewal within 4 or 8-hour windows
- **Automation:** Maximize use of automated plant and equipment for replacement/renewal[^1]

**Why HS2 Chose Slab Track:**[^10]

- Ballasted track life at **60+ MGTPA** would require renewal every **~20 years**
- Tamping requirements excessive at 330-360 km/h speeds
- **Hand-back at >300 km/h not considered feasible** even with improved DTS[^10]
- Whole-life cost, carbon footprint, and disruption considerations favored slab track[^10]


### 7.2 ADIF Maintenance Approach

**Maintenance Strategy:**

- Proactive monitoring using track recording cars
- **Neobalast innovation:** ADIF developing "disaggregated ballast with improved durability and resistance to fragmentation"[^11]
- Mechanized tamping using modern high-output machines
- Ballast cleaning cycles based on contamination monitoring[^12]
- Predictive maintenance using track geometry data

**Inspection:**[^12]

- Regular ballast inspection to ensure load distribution
- Fixed and immovable sleeper position verification
- **Philosophy:** "The ballast distributes the loads transmitted by the trains evenly on the platform"[^12]


### 7.3 Network Rail Maintenance Strategy

**Maintenance Regime:**[^7]

- **Design life (ballasted track):** ~25 years (high-speed main line), up to 50 years (secondary)[^7]
- **Tamping cycles:** Variable based on tonnage and track geometry deterioration rate[^7]
- **Ballast cleaning:** When GPR and geometry data indicate contamination levels critical
- **Track bed investigation:** Comprehensive GPR surveys, BFI (Ballast Fouling Index) analysis[^7]

**Innovation:**[^7]

- **Bitumen asphalt sub-ballast trials:** Following HS2 research to improve ballasted track performance[^6]
- **Under Sleeper Pad deployment:** Increasing use to extend tamping intervals
- **Ground improvement techniques:** Piling, grouting for problem areas identified through GPR[^7]

**Comparison Summary - Maintenance:**


| Parameter | HS2 | ADIF | Network Rail |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Design life (ballasted)** | 20 years @ >60 MGTPA | Variable | 25-50 years depending on route |
| **Primary trackform choice** | Slab track (ballasted not feasible) | Mixed (ballasted for most routes) | Ballasted (slab for specific applications) |
| **Maintenance window** | 5 hrs (night) / 8 hrs (weekend) | Variable | Variable (possessions required) |
| **Automation level** | Maximum automation target | High mechanization | High mechanization |
| **Innovation focus** | Optimal initial design | Neobalast development | Bitumen sub-ballast, USPs |

## 8. Key Philosophical Differences

### 8.1 HS2: Future-Proofed, Minimal Maintenance Philosophy

**Core Principles:**[^10][^1]

- **"Design out maintenance"** approach - minimize intervention need through optimal initial specification
- **60-year design life** minimum for system (excluding rail)
- **Very high initial capital investment** justified by whole-life cost analysis
- **~60 MGTPA traffic levels** (among world's highest high-speed loadings) drive requirements[^10]
- **Predominantly slab track** because ballasted track maintenance considered infeasible at operational speeds and tonnages[^10]
- **Bitumen asphalt sub-ballast** innovation where ballasted track used[^6]
- **Category A ballast (LA ≤14)** - world's most stringent specification[^1]

**Critical Design Speed:**[^6][^1]

- **360 km/h operational** (400 km/h where geometry permits)
- At these speeds with high tonnage, ballasted track would require:
    - Excessive tamping (degrading ballast life rapidly)
    - ~20-year ballast renewal cycles (considered too disruptive)[^10]
    - Hand-back >300 km/h "not considered feasible"[^10]


### 8.2 ADIF: Balanced Performance and Pragmatism

**Core Principles:**[^4][^2]

- **Proven technology adaptation** - European best practice with Spanish adaptations
- **Three-tier ballast specification** allows optimization by line type and speed[^4]
- **Flexible maintenance** - mechanized approaches suited to Spanish network density
- **Innovation in materials** (Neobalast research) to extend ballast life[^11]
- **Mixed trackforms:** Ballasted for most routes, slab for specific high-demand sections
- **UIC methodology foundation** with safety factors for high-speed applications[^2]

**Design Philosophy:**[^2]

- Incremental improvements to proven ballasted track technology
- **Cost-effectiveness** balanced against performance requirements
- **Adaptable specifications** - different requirements for different line classes
- Focus on extending asset life through improved materials rather than wholesale trackform change


### 8.3 Network Rail: Asset Management and Continuous Improvement

**Core Principles:**[^3][^7]

- **Legacy infrastructure management** - extensive existing network with varied age and condition
- **Risk-based asset management** - target interventions where most needed[^7]
- **Learning from international best practice** - HS2 innovations feeding back to Network Rail[^6]
- **Pragmatic approach** - balance performance, cost, and operational disruption
- **Data-driven decision making** - GPR, geometry monitoring, predictive analytics[^7]

**Evolution:**[^6]

- **Learning from HS2:** Bitumen asphalt sub-ballast trials for high-speed routes[^6]
- **USP deployment increasing** to extend tamping cycles
- **Track bed total route evaluation** approach for renewals and asset management[^7]
- **"Right track in right place"** philosophy - optimize trackform for specific route requirements

## 10. Key Takeaways for Permanent Way Engineers

1. **HS2 represents the cutting edge** of ballasted/slab track engineering for ultra-high-speed, ultra-high-tonnage applications. Its specifications are **the most stringent globally** but come with very high capital costs justified only by extreme operational demands.
2. **ADIF demonstrates pragmatic excellence** - applying European best practice (UIC methodologies) with Spanish adaptations, using tiered specifications to optimize cost-performance across diverse network types.
3. **Network Rail shows effective legacy network management** - balancing performance improvement with asset condition reality, increasingly adopting innovations (bitumen sub-ballast, USPs) from HS2 research where justified.
4. **Ballast specifications convergence:** All three use LA ≤14 for highest-speed applications, demonstrating international consensus on material quality requirements for demanding applications.
5. **USP technology now mainstream:** All three administrators increasingly mandate or specify Under Sleeper Pads, recognizing benefits for track geometry stability and maintenance cycle extension.
6. **Sub-ballast innovation:** HS2's bitumen asphalt sub-ballast represents significant innovation being watched closely (and adopted) by ADIF and Network Rail as potential ballasted track performance enhancer.
7. **Track stiffness management critical:** All three recognize importance of consistent, optimized stiffness (neither too soft nor too hard) - HS2 specifies most tightly (100±20 kN/mm), others use performance-based approaches.
8. **Maintenance philosophy divergence:** HS2 "designs out" maintenance through capital-intensive slab track; ADIF balances ballasted track optimization with selective slab use; Network Rail focuses on extending ballasted track life through targeted improvements.
9. **Standards hierarchy:** All work within European framework (EN standards) but apply administrator-specific requirements reflecting operational contexts: HS2 (new-build, ultra-performance), ADIF (high-speed network expansion), Network Rail (mixed legacy/modern infrastructure).
10. **Future direction:** Trend toward higher-specification ballasted track (better materials, USPs, improved sub-ballast) for speeds up to ~250-300 km/h, with transition to slab track at higher speeds/tonnages where whole-life costs justify capital investment.
